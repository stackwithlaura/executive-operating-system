# Executive Dashboard Framework

> A concise leadership view of the information required to understand performance, identify risks, make decisions, and maintain organizational alignment.

---

## Purpose

An executive dashboard should help leadership answer five questions quickly:

1. Are we making progress against our most important goals?
2. What has changed since the last review?
3. Where are we at risk?
4. What decisions are needed?
5. What requires leadership attention now?

The dashboard is not intended to display every available metric.

Its purpose is to create clarity.

---

## Operating Principles

### Focus on business outcomes

Measure progress against strategic priorities rather than reporting activity for activity's sake.

### Show signal, not noise

Include only the information leadership needs to understand performance or make a decision.

### Add context to every metric

A number without a target, trend, or explanation is not useful.

### Surface problems early

The dashboard should make risk visible before it becomes a crisis.

### Assign ownership

Every priority, risk, and action should have a clearly identified owner.

### Maintain a consistent rhythm

Use the same structure and definitions during every review so changes are easy to identify.

---

## Recommended Dashboard Structure

### 1. Executive Summary

A brief summary of the current state of the business or function.

Include:

- Overall status
- Most important development
- Most significant win
- Most urgent concern
- Primary focus for the next reporting period

---

### 2. Strategic Priorities

Track the limited number of priorities that matter most.

| Strategic Priority | Desired Outcome | Owner | Status | Progress | Next Milestone |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Priority 1 | Define the intended result | Name or role | On Track / At Risk / Off Track | Brief update | Next meaningful checkpoint |
| Priority 2 | Define the intended result | Name or role | On Track / At Risk / Off Track | Brief update | Next meaningful checkpoint |
| Priority 3 | Define the intended result | Name or role | On Track / At Risk / Off Track | Brief update | Next meaningful checkpoint |

A dashboard should generally contain no more than three to five strategic priorities.

---

### 3. Key Performance Indicators

Track the measures that best reflect business performance.

| KPI | Current | Target | Prior Period | Trend | Commentary |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---|---|
| KPI 1 | — | — | — | Improving / Stable / Declining | Explain the movement |
| KPI 2 | — | — | — | Improving / Stable / Declining | Explain the movement |
| KPI 3 | — | — | — | Improving / Stable / Declining | Explain the movement |

Each KPI should have:

- A clear definition
- A reliable source
- A reporting owner
- A target or benchmark
- A consistent reporting cadence
- A reason it matters to the business

---

### 4. Wins and Momentum

Capture meaningful progress, not routine completed tasks.

Examples include:

- Revenue or pipeline milestones
- Customer expansion
- Major customer outcomes
- Operational improvements
- Cost savings
- Product adoption
- Strategic partnerships
- Brand or market recognition
- Important team accomplishments

---

### 5. Risks and Constraints

Identify issues that could affect strategic priorities or business performance.

| Risk or Constraint | Potential Impact | Likelihood | Owner | Mitigation | Status |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Risk 1 | Describe the business consequence | Low / Medium / High | Name or role | Planned response | Open / Monitoring / Resolved |

Risks should be specific enough to support action.

Avoid vague statements such as:

- Communication needs improvement
- Resources are limited
- The market is challenging

Instead state:

- Customer story production is delayed because field approvals do not have an assigned owner.
- Pipeline reporting is incomplete because lead-source definitions are inconsistent across systems.

---

### 6. Decisions Needed

List decisions that require leadership input or approval.

| Decision | Why It Matters | Recommendation | Decision Owner | Needed By |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Decision 1 | Describe the impact | State the recommended path | Name or role | Date |

This section prevents important decisions from becoming buried inside status updates.

---

### 7. Cross-Functional Dependencies

Document work that depends on another team, leader, system, or external partner.

| Dependency | Teams Involved | Impact | Required Action | Owner |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Dependency 1 | Team A / Team B | Describe what is affected | Define the next action | Name or role |

---

### 8. Next-Period Focus

Identify the three most important outcomes for the next reporting period.

1. Outcome one
2. Outcome two
3. Outcome three

These should reflect business priorities, not a complete task list.

---

## Suggested Status Definitions

### On Track

The priority is expected to meet its target without additional intervention.

### At Risk

The target may be missed unless an issue is resolved or additional action is taken.

### Off Track

The target is unlikely to be met under the current plan.

### Complete

The intended outcome has been achieved and verified.

### Monitoring

No immediate action is required, but continued observation is necessary.

---

## Review Cadence

The appropriate cadence depends on the business and the dashboard's purpose.

### Weekly

Best for:

- Immediate priorities
- Emerging risks
- Decisions
- Pipeline movement
- Fast-moving execution

### Monthly

Best for:

- KPI trends
- Budget performance
- Strategic initiative progress
- Customer and market insights
- Cross-functional alignment

### Quarterly

Best for:

- Strategy
- Resource allocation
- Organizational performance
- Major investments
- Roadmap changes

A dashboard may be updated weekly while receiving a more comprehensive monthly or quarterly review.

---

## Dashboard Design Standards

A useful executive dashboard should be:

- Brief enough to review quickly
- Consistent from period to period
- Built around decisions and outcomes
- Supported by reliable data
- Clear about ownership
- Honest about risks
- Free from unnecessary detail
- Adaptable to the needs of the organization

Where possible, the main dashboard should fit on one page or one screen.

Supporting detail can be linked separately.

---

## Questions to Ask Before Adding a Metric

Before placing anything on the dashboard, ask:

1. What business question does this answer?
2. Who will use this information?
3. What decision could it influence?
4. Is the data reliable?
5. Does it have a target or meaningful comparison?
6. Will we act differently based on the result?
7. Is this already represented elsewhere?

If the metric does not support understanding, accountability, or action, it probably does not belong on the executive dashboard.

---

## Anti-Patterns

Avoid dashboards that:

- Contain every available metric
- Report activity without business context
- Hide poor performance behind averages
- Use inconsistent definitions
- Change structure every reporting period
- Present information without ownership
- Identify problems without proposing next steps
- Require a lengthy explanation to understand
- Exist only because leadership requested “a dashboard”

---

## Framework Outcome

When used effectively, the Executive Dashboard should create:

- Shared understanding
- Faster decision-making
- Clear accountability
- Earlier identification of risk
- Better cross-functional alignment
- Stronger focus on strategic outcomes

> The dashboard is not the work. It is the instrument that helps leaders understand whether the work is producing the intended result.
